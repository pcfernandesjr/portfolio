Designing to engage and present technically complex products




Disclaimer: Since I worked under NDA clausules, I am not allowed to present mockups delivered to clients. However, to have something to present, I redesigned some layouts, also doing some adjustments and blurrying out sensible information.



Virtualization and Cloud computing solutions can become overwhelming or confusing for most of the people to understand.

Our client is among the biggest players in this industry, and willing to make potential customers know their products better, they first commissioned a test-drive portal where accredited users can try their products in already configured test environments. The next step was providing a way to provide an improved learning experience by offering tutorials and focused tasks together with the product demos.


Challenges

Our client is a big company with a great amount of internal competition, which means that we need to comply to decision makers from several origins. Units that might find interesting to have their solution featured in, helping generate awareness and more business opportunities are also key players on this.

While the product’s vision was being detailed and matured, the UX team and I started working on some wireframes, so it could be easier to visualize if we were going into the right direction or not, also facilitating the process of finding problems and issues that we wouldn’t be able to foresee otherwise.



In the end, this project’s objective is still to design a tool to generate sales. We needed to balance the experience enough to still keep this objective and not drift too far into making it too similar to an e-learning platform. Oh, and of course the biggest question: how to make this an appealing experience to users?


Solutions

We decided to invest time into organizing brainstorm sessions with teammates from different areas, back-end, front-end, testing and support. This diverse environment was important to gather a great amount of insights and technical point of views that would help us solidify the upcoming design decisions.

With the wireframing phase completed, we started to switch focus to the visual design. I helped with researching options for a a grid system, deciding in the end to follow 4 breakpoint widths (>1280px, 960px, 640px and 320px) and a base of 4 vertical spacing and component size. By selecting some screens that would use the most visual components to begin with, we had our starting point to create our design system.



After defining the design system, we started producing the screen mockups, by having a common library created on Adobe XD, we could design pages as a team without straying from a unified visual style. In order to guide the developers I also created some screen flows so they could associate the pages with their corresponding layouts.
